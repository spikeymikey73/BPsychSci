Week 1: Introduction to Social Psychology: What is Social Psychology?

How is social psychology defined and how is it similar / different to other research areas?

The scientific investigation of how the thoughts, feelings and behaviours of individuals are influenced by the actual, imagined or implied presence of others.

How can the content within social psychology be applied?

The primary applications of social psychology address questions involving our social perceptions, influence and relations with others. These questions can be used to inform all sorts of relational settings such as universities, medicine, law, business, government agencies and the military to name a few. All applications examine our attitudes and behaviours in a social group or a society at large.

Is social psychology just ‘common sense’? Why are psychologists interested in studying what most view as ‘common sense’?

Differences between social psychology and traditional folk wisdom:

False: Beauty and brains do not mix. False: People think they are more unique than they are. False: Sport and Video games are a release for aggression. False: Being paid more means that people enjoy their work more.

We are unable to answer the folksy questions alone:

  • Opposites attract vs. Birds of a feather flock together.
  • Two heads are better than one vs. Too many cooks spoil the broth.

Social Psychology empirically tests these ‘common sense’ theories. They are emotionally compelling, but not empirically tested. They can be completely false or misleading and often have multiple answers.

What are levels of analysis (or explaination) and why is it important to social psychology?

Levels of aggression (for example) can be examined in multiple ways:

Intrapersonal: This level of analysis is within the person, such as how we make hostile attributions about another person. Interpersonal/Situational: A level of analysis between people within a specific situation, such as how people might react when they are insulted by a stranger. Positional: A level of analysis between people in specific situations, but with their position outside the situation considered, such as how a person of low or high status might react when they are insulted by a stranger. Ideological: A level of analysis between people including social beliefs and social relationships between groups. For example, wider social norms influence how a person reacts when insulted by a stranger.

How does social psychology differentiate between cognition, affect and behaviour?

Affect: emotions/feelings Behaviour: interactions/actions Cognition: thought

Social psychologists are more interested in the social interaction or situation in their research and its effect on people rather than the individual only. The three capacities above help to predict social interactions (which are individualistic) to differing degrees, however, we do not always know which combinations of these an individual may employ to deal with the given situation.

What is the belonging hypothesis and what role does it play in social psychology?

The Belonging Hypothesis or our need for affiliation is a basic concept in social psychology (as we can all relate to it), or a universal experience.

We have a ‘basic drive to form and maintain at least a minimum quantity of lasting, positive and significant interpersonal relationships’ regardless of who we are or where we are from. Examples of this include wearing a uniform, joining a club, belonging to a nation or religion.

Acceptance and belonging predicts higher levels of happiness (more so than gender, income or religion) with rejection, ostracism and exclusion are universal predictors of lonliness, depression or poor health.

How is ostracism defined, and what are the major findings regarding this concept?

Ostracism is defined as a lack of fulfilment of our basic social need to belong, or deliberate exclusion from groups.

The major findings regarding ostracism are:

  • Physiological and emotional pain (fMRI scans showing activity brain centres related to pain, namely the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex and anterior insula).
  • Ostracism imposes a threat to our emotional needs:
    • belonging
    • self-esteem
    • control over our environment
    • finding meaning in what we are doing
  • Ostracism has a basis in an adaptive alert system (both prosocial and antisocial)
  • Those high in social anxiety often find it difficult to recover from perceived ostracism or instances of antisocial alerts provided by these adaptive alerts from social groups.

What are currently the major perspectives of social psychology?

How has emotion and motivation been treated previously, and how have these concepts become integrated into social psychology research?

Emotion and motivation had previously been ignored with greater emphasis placed on cognition. Today’s research methods integrate emotion, motivation AND cognition to investigate the unconscious elements of social interaction taking biases into account. Other unconscious influences can include stereotypes and other automatic impulses.

How are evolutionary perspectives used in social psychology?

Evolutionary perspectives in social psychology investigate how emotions like jealousy impact us in today’s world, but also consider the functional purpose/s jealousy may have had for our ancestors. These perspectives may also look into the different purposes of jealousy based on gender, as well as different social situations that could trigger jealousy.

What is culture defined? What is cross-cultural and multicultural research, and how do they differ?

Culture: A system of enduring meanings, beliefs, values, assumptions, institutions and practices shared by a large group of people and passed on to the next generation.

Coss-cultural research: Research designed to compare and contrast people of a variety of different cultures.

Multicultural research: Research designed to examine racial and ethnic groups within a society built out of many different cultures.

What is behavioural economics and how is it related to social psychology?

Behavioural economics is a field examining how other disciplines like social and cognitive psychology influence economic decision making.

What ia social neuroscience and what research has been conducted in the field?

Social Neuroscience looks at how our actions affect our neural processes such as playing violent video games, or how different patterns of brain activity affect our perceptions of other cultural groups.

Why are social psychologists interested in studying new technologies and the online world?

New technologies allow us to investigate the brain more deeply (and less intrusively!) though measuring and recording brain activity in the following ways:

  • Event related Potential (ERP)
  • Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)
  • Magnetoencephalography (MEG)
  • Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI)

The online world is constantly evolving, as are the ways in which we use it to live, work and interact. The impact this world has on us is constantly being researched (cognitive benefit of playing video games)

Week 3: Social Cognition and Perception: How do we think about the social world?

What are the major theoretical perspectives of social cognition, and what assumptions do they hold?

Define the terms ‘social cognition’ and ‘social perception’, and outline how they differ.

Social Cognition refers to the model that we have built in our minds about how to interact with others socially, whether it be our expectations about their behaviour or our own mode of interacting with them. It is the automated “program” that unconsciously dictates our behaviours.

Social Perception refers to our observations of behaviours (both our own and that of others) and attributions of causal factors that motivate someone to behave a certain way. Internal Attributions look at the reasons a person behaves the way they do based on their internal dialogue, whereas External Attributions seek to explain the situation a person is in that leads them to behave a certain way.

The difference between these two is that SC is an unconscious model of our world and the people in it, whereas SP looks at the way we store a particular event within our cognitive model.

Outline the major assumptions of the following broad approaches to social cognition, as found in social psychology: (a) cognitive consistency; (b) naive scientist; (c) cognitive miser; and (d) motivated tactician.

Cognitive Consistency: A theory devised in the 40s and 50s that assumes people alter their attitudes and beliefs about the world in order to ensure that their mental processes are consistent or align neatly with each other.

Naive Scientist: This theory is the foundation of attribution, suggesting that we assign meaning to the thoughts and actions of others, by assuming that we are all thoughtful and rational.

Cognitive Miser: The cognitive miser theories suggested that we are not rational when considering the motivations of others, and that we jump to conclusions instead as a way of minimising the energy we expend on analysis of behaviours.

Motivated Technician: This theory looked into the multiple strategies that a person may engage in to meet goals, motives and needs. On some occasions we choose wisely, and on others we are defensive and seek to protect our self esteem.

What is impression formation, and what key factors contribute to impression formation?

What are central and peripheral traits? Provide an example of each.

Central traits are those which have a profound influence on our impressions of a person (such as angry/happy), whereas peripheral traits are those which have only a brief or passing influence on our impression of others (such as polite/blunt).

What did Asch do to test his ideas and what were his findings?

To test his theory, Asch gave two separate groups a list of slightly differing traits belonging to an imaginary person. One list contained the word warm and the other list contained the word cold (being central traits). The participants who received the list warmth as a character trait formed a more favourable impression of the person than the group who received the list containing the word cold. These words were then replaced with polite and blunt (peripheral traits), with the different perceptions from each group being much closer together.

What criticisms have been made regarding the concept of central trait? (Note the role of personal constructs and implicit personality theories).

Critics of this theory have suggested that central traits are influenced by context, and can be considered more central where other confirmatory traits could be linked. It is further suggested that people have their own unique methods for characterising people differing from that of others. personal constructs

Week 4: Emotions and Affect: How do we feel about the social world?

What is the underlying physiology of emotions, including the role of the sympathetic nervous system (SNS), the parasympathetic nervous system (PNS), and the amygdala?

The SNS is the arousal division of the autonomic nervous system responsible for readying our bodies to threats by activating our endocrine system. Some responses include heightened heart rate, slowing digestion, dilating pupils (widening our field of vision), releasing sugar from the liver (for energy) and inhibiting the PNS.

The PNS is responsible for resting, digesting, relaxing and recovering.

The amygdala is part of the limbic system responsible for mediating the function of the SNS and PNS to change between these two states of lowered or heightened alertness.

How is arousal seen (when the SNS is activated) important to emotions?

The activated state of the SNS and its changes to our bodily sensations is an indication of strong emotions.

What is the amygdala, and what role does it play in emotions?

The amygdala is a part of our limbic system tgat helps us experience emotions. It regulates our interpretations of fear and aggression, inhibiting our PNS and activating our SNS through the release of endocrine.

What is the difference between basic and secondary emotions, and what function does each play?

Define ‘basic emotions’ and outline their major characteristics.

Basic Emotions: anger, contempt, disgust, fear, happiness, sadness, surprise, etc.

These emotions are primarily based on the arousal function of the SNS and happen quickly (direct processing), inducing the inhibition of the PNS. There is almost no logical processing of these emotions.

What were the findings of Ekman et al. regarding emotions? What are the major conclusions we can take away from these studies?

Ekman studied the expression and interpretation of these emotions across a large number of different cultures to find them almost constant regardless of culture. There were 162 of these emotions tested in his study.

Define secondary emotions and outline their characteristics.

These emotions result in lower SNS arousal, and are primarily cognitive in nature as there is little threat or immense pleasure associated with them. (i.e., guilt, shame, embarrassment).

What impact does arousal and misattributing arousal have on our emotions?

arousal + cognition = emotion

A number of experiments have been conducted into the misattribution of arousal, where participants are induced into a state of arousal via various methods and controls (placebo, misinformation, injections of epinephrine, etc).

These experiments confirm the two factor theory of emotion as per the formula above. Misattribution can occur as a result of some type of situational factor where the associated emotion is ‘misattributed’. We are often not directly able to label our emotional processes correctly, highlighting the power of suggestion to inform us of the source. Cognitive dissonance is a potential explanation for this, where discomfort is experienced as a result of holding two or more opposing beliefs or attitudes. Suggestions of other misattributions could be to avoid a cup of coffee prior to a job interview to reduce the likelihood of nervousness before an interview, or taking a date to a horror movie to cause them to misattribute their racing heart as attraction.

What role do emotions play in the intrapersonal, interpersonal and socio-cultural context? Provide examples.

Emotions can be an important ingredient in relationships, as they communicate to others our attraction, disappointment, happiness, etc. They can also help us understand others, which in turn can strengthen a relationship.

We sometimes make efforts to regulate or suppress our emotions. Regulation may be handled a few different ways:

  1. Change the emotion we feel (bad to good)
  2. Postpone it to another time (when we’re alone), or
  3. Change the way the emotion is experienced or expressed.

Most research focusses on reappraisal or suppression.

Habitual suppression, however, can negatively impact social connections by weakening them, whereas reappraisal can help strengthen our social connections.

Some of the effects of habitual suppression can be:

  • Cause negative first impressions,
  • lower social support and lower social / relational quality.

Problems with suppression include:

  • The constant regulation needed to suppress can prevent us from perceiving an interaction correctly,
  • Leads us to feel inauthentic / or be preceived as inauthentic.

Some suppression can be healthy for relationships (tact) but regulation is more effective as it is adaptive and can lead to solving problems in a constructive way.

  • Prepare out body for immediate action (fight, flight, etc.)
  • Emotions influence thoughts (neural links to our memories)
  • Emotions motivate future behaviours (seeking out positive experiences, avoiding negative experiences)
